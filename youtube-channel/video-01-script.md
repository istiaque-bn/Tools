## SCRIPT

### HOOK (0:00–0:30)

Here's something nobody tells you before you move to America: the moment you land, you are financially invisible. It doesn't matter if you had a perfect banking record back home, a great salary, savings in the bank. In the US system, you don't exist. And that invisible wall will cost you real money — higher deposits on your apartment, rejection for a car loan, sometimes even a worse phone plan. Today I'll show you exactly how to go from invisible to a solid credit score, step by step — even if you don't have a Social Security Number yet.

### WHY CREDIT MATTERS HERE (0:30–1:45)

Quick context, because the US is unusual. Almost everything financial here runs on a three-digit number between 300 and 850 called your credit score. Landlords check it. Lenders check it. Some employers check a version of it. It's calculated from your credit history — a record of how you've borrowed and repaid money in the US specifically. Your history from home doesn't transfer. That's the trap: you can't get credit without history, and you can't get history without credit. Except — there are doors built specifically for people in your situation. Let's open them.

### STEP 1 — GET YOUR TAX ID SORTED (1:45–3:00)

First: identification. If you're eligible for a Social Security Number through your visa, get it — it makes everything easier. But if you're not eligible, you are not locked out. You can apply to the IRS for an ITIN — an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number. It exists for exactly this: people who need to interact with the US financial system but don't qualify for an SSN. The standard IRS process takes roughly 7 to 11 weeks, so start early. Several banks and card issuers will accept an ITIN, or even a passport plus visa, where others demand an SSN. Write this down: SSN if you can, ITIN if you can't, and don't pay anyone who promises to "sell" you a shortcut.

### STEP 2 — OPEN A US BANK ACCOUNT (3:00–3:45)

Before credit, you need banking. Open a checking account at a major bank — most will accept a passport plus proof of address, and many accept ITINs. This doesn't build credit by itself, but every credit application will go smoother with a US account behind it, and you'll need it to pay your future card bill. Pro tip: some banks have newcomer programs designed for exactly this — ask specifically.

### STEP 3 — GET A SECURED CREDIT CARD (3:45–5:30)

Now the engine of this whole plan: the secured credit card. Here's how it works. You give the bank a refundable deposit — usually somewhere between fifty and a few hundred dollars — and that deposit becomes your credit limit. Because the bank holds your money as collateral, they'll approve people with zero history. You use the card for small purchases, you pay it off, and here's the magic: every month, that payment gets reported to the credit bureaus — the companies that build your credit file. Congratulations, you now exist.

Major issuers like Capital One and Discover offer secured cards, and several accept ITINs. There are also newer companies built specifically for immigrants and international students that will approve you with a passport and visa — no SSN, no US history at all. I'll put examples in the description.

Two rules that decide whether this works or backfires. Rule one: pay the full balance, on time, every single month. One late payment on a thin file is a crater. Rule two: don't use more than about 30% of your limit. If your limit is $200, keep the balance under $60. Low utilization plus perfect payments — that's the entire formula.

### STEP 4 — STACK THE EXTRAS (5:30–7:00)

While the secured card does the heavy lifting, add these accelerators. First: become an authorized user. If you have a family member or trusted friend with a good US credit card, they can add you to their account — their good history starts helping your file, and they don't even have to give you the physical card. Second: rent reporting. Your rent is probably your biggest monthly payment and normally it counts for nothing. Third-party services can report it to the bureaus so it builds your file. Third: a credit-builder loan from a credit union — you "borrow" money that sits locked in an account while your payments get reported, and you get the money at the end. None of these are mandatory. Each one speeds up the clock.

### STEP 5 — THE 6-TO-12-MONTH TIMELINE (7:00–8:15)

So what should you expect? Roughly six months of on-time payments before you have a usable score at all — that's how long the scoring models need. By month six to twelve, with perfect payments and low utilization, many people land somewhere around 650 to 700+. That's when doors open: your secured deposit often gets refunded and the card converts to a regular one, better cards start approving you, and apartment applications stop being humiliating. This is a 12-month project, not a 12-day one — but it's the most predictable investment you'll ever make. You literally cannot lose if you follow the two rules.

### RECAP + CTA (8:15–9:00)

Recap: get your SSN or ITIN. Open a bank account. Get a secured card and treat it like a tool, not free money — full payment every month, under 30% used. Stack authorized user status and rent reporting if you can. Then let time do its job.

Quick disclaimer: I'm sharing education, not financial advice — check current terms before applying to anything.
